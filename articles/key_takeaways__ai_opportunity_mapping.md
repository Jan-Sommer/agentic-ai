# Key Takeaways: AI Opportunity Mapping
*INNOQ*

## The Core Idea
Most organizations accumulate AI initiatives driven by FOMO rather than strategy. The result is a long
list of ideas with no clear basis for choosing what to build first. AI Opportunity Mapping is a
structured method for turning that list into 2–3 prioritized, actionable use cases — evaluated on
impact, feasibility, and data availability — so decisions have a defensible foundation.

## What We Should Take Away

### 1. FOMO Is a Poor Compass
The most common failure mode in enterprise AI adoption is not moving too slowly — it is moving in
too many directions at once. Accumulating projects without evaluation of which ones genuinely
deliver value produces initiative sprawl, wasted effort, and no clear wins. Strategic focus means
explicitly choosing what *not* to pursue as much as what to build.

### 2. Three Axes Determine Whether a Use Case Is Worth Pursuing
Every AI use case should be evaluated against three independent dimensions before development begins:

| Axis | The question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Impact** | What value does this create — for users, for teams, for the business? Is it measurable? |
| **Feasibility** | Can this actually be built with available technology, skills, and timeline? |
| **Data availability** | Is the required data accessible, in sufficient quality and quantity? |

A use case that scores well on impact but poorly on data availability is a Phase 3 candidate at
best — it requires data foundation work first. A use case that is highly feasible but low impact is
not worth the investment. All three axes must be evaluated together.

### 3. The AI Opportunity Mapping Canvas Structures the Process
The canvas is a structured tool (similar in spirit to the Business Model Canvas) that makes use cases
comparable. Five interconnected areas:

| Area | Question |
|---|---|
| **Domain** | In which operational area or process is this use case? |
| **Journey** | What problems or inefficiencies exist today? |
| **Ideation** | Which AI approaches could address this? |
| **Impact** | What value would the solution create — for users, teams, or the business? |
| **Roadmap** | What are realistic next steps to test or implement? |

The canvas forces teams to think through all five areas for every candidate before comparing use
cases. Incomplete thinking on one area (particularly Impact and Roadmap) is often what separates
ideas that sound good from use cases that can actually be built and measured.

### 4. Use Case Discovery Requires Cross-Functional Participation
Technically sound AI ideas that ignore business context fail in practice. Organizationally framed
ideas without technical feasibility input result in plans that can't be built. The right team for
AI opportunity mapping combines: executives or department heads (strategic framing), product and
process owners (workflow knowledge), UX/design (user impact), data owners (data availability),
and IT/architecture (feasibility and integration). None of these perspectives is optional — their
absence produces a blind spot in the evaluation.

### 5. The Output Is a Prioritized Shortlist, Not a Wishlist
The goal of the process is not to document all possible AI applications. It is to identify the 2–3
use cases with the highest combined score across impact, feasibility, and data availability — and
to produce a concrete implementation roadmap for those, not a backlog for future consideration.
This distinction matters: a wishlist diffuses attention; a prioritized shortlist with a roadmap
enables execution.

## The Bottom Line
The structured evaluation of AI use cases — against impact, feasibility, and data availability —
is what separates initiatives that produce results from ones that produce activity. Organizations
that skip this step tend to build what is technically interesting or politically visible rather
than what is most valuable. The canvas and the three-axis evaluation framework are reusable:
they apply not only to initial prioritization but to any future decision about adding a new AI
capability to an existing roadmap.
